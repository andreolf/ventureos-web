Ask most funds what their "stack" is and you'll hear: a CRM nobody fully trusts, a shared drive, a few spreadsheets, and a lot of email. It works because smart people paper over the gaps with effort. But effort doesn't scale, and the gaps are exactly where deals leak out.

The modern stack replaces that patchwork with a single **intelligence layer** — one system that sources, organizes, evaluates, and moves.

## The four layers

**1. Sourcing.** Proactive, not just reactive. The stack watches leading signals and brings ranked opportunities to the team, instead of waiting for inbound.

**2. Evaluation.** First-pass memos, market research, and call prep are generated automatically from a company's materials — in the fund's own format. Partners edit and decide; they don't transcribe.

**3. Relationships.** Email and calendar flow into the system automatically, so the relationship graph is always current. No one "updates the CRM" — it updates itself.

**4. Pipeline.** Every deal, every stage, one view — with nudges when something goes stale. The system remembers so the team doesn't have to.

## Why "one layer" beats best-of-breed

You could buy a sourcing tool, a memo tool, a CRM, and an email plugin. But value compounds when they share context. A memo is better when it can see the founder's commit history. Sourcing is better when it knows what you passed on and why. Stitching four vendors together loses exactly the context that makes AI useful.

## The compounding moat

The real reason to rebuild now: **your data becomes your edge.** Every deal logged, every pass reason recorded, every founder tracked sharpens a system tuned to *your* thesis. A fund that starts building this layer today isn't just more efficient this quarter — it's accumulating a proprietary intelligence asset competitors can't purchase off the shelf.

The firms that win the next decade won't be the ones with the most analysts. They'll be the ones whose stack makes each analyst count for ten.
