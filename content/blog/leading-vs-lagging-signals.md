There's a comfortable trap in venture data: it feels productive to track fundraises. A new dashboard lights up, a company raised a seed, you add it to a list. But think about what that signal actually tells you: **the round is done.** The company chose its investors. You're reading the obituary of an opportunity you never had.

That's a *lagging* signal. Useful for market maps and comps — useless for getting in.

## The distinction that matters

- **Lagging signals** describe events that have already resolved: fundraises, acquisitions, "emerges from stealth with $X." By the time they're visible, the decision has been made.
- **Leading signals** describe activity *in progress*: a founder shipping code, a product launching, a waitlist filling, an operator quietly leaving a strong company. The outcome is still open — which means you can still influence it.

An early-stage fund's entire edge lives in the leading column.

## Why systems drift toward lagging signals anyway

Because they're easy. Fundraises are announced, structured, and public. Leading signals are messy, unstructured, and require judgment to interpret. So most tools optimize for what's easy to ingest — and quietly become market-mapping products dressed up as sourcing products.

A sourcing system earns its keep only if it does the hard thing: watch the messy, early surfaces and resist the temptation to just re-report the news.

## Building around leading signals

Three principles:

1. **Weight by earliness.** A commit history should outrank a press mention. Downrank anything that describes a closed event.
2. **Make every signal actionable.** A signal you can't act on is trivia. Each one should come with a path to outreach — a profile, a repo, a warm-intro route.
3. **Guard against staleness.** Explicitly detect and demote "already closed" language so lagging signals never masquerade as opportunities.

Do this and your pipeline stops being a rear-view mirror and starts being a windshield.
