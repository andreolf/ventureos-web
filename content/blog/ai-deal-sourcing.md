Most funds source deals two ways: inbound (a founder emails you) and network (someone you trust makes an intro). Both are good. Both are also *reactive* — you find out about a company when someone decides to tell you, which is usually when they're already raising and talking to everyone else too.

AI-driven sourcing adds a third channel that's **proactive**: it watches the places founders show up *before* they announce anything, and brings them to you.

## Where early founders actually are

Long before a founder has a deck, they leave a trail:

- **Code.** A technical founder is pushing commits to a new repo — often months before there's a company, let alone a round. Location markers and activity spikes are detectable.
- **Launches.** Product Hunt, BetaList, and waitlist pages surface products at the exact moment they go public — pre-revenue, pre-raise.
- **Writing.** Founders articulate their thinking on X and in newsletters before they have a website. The thesis comes before the pitch.

None of these require the founder to know you exist. That's the point.

## Why this beats the firehose

Inbound scales badly. Double your inbound and you double the triage burden without improving hit rate. AI sourcing scales the *opposite* way: the system reads everything, scores it against your thesis, and hands your team a short, ranked list of the few worth a human look.

The output isn't "more deals." It's **higher-signal deals, earlier** — with enough lead time to build a relationship before the round is competitive.

## Sourcing is a ranking problem, not a search problem

The hard part was never finding companies — it's deciding which of the thousands matter to *you*. That's where scoring against your specific thesis (stage, geography, sector, signal type) turns a firehose into a pipeline.

Get the ranking right and your team spends its scarcest resource — attention — only on what deserves it.
